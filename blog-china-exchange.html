Two Weeks in Shenzhen: The Exchange That Changed My Perspective

March 2026 · 7 min read

I've been to a few countries before. I've seen different cultures, tried different foods, met different people. But nothing — and I mean nothing — prepared me for two weeks in Shenzhen, China. This wasn't just a trip. This was, without exaggeration, one of the highlights of my last few years.

The exchange was organized through Bosch in partnership with the Shenzhen Institute of Information Technology. Two weeks on campus, staying in a hotel right on university grounds. The official purpose: get a drone pilot license. The real outcome: something much bigger than that.

The Drone License

Let's start with the technical part. The program was structured around obtaining a certified drone pilot license — theory sessions on regulations, airspace rules, and flight safety, followed by hands-on practical training with actual drones. The theory was dense but well-taught, and the practical sessions were where it got exciting. There's something about actually flying a drone through an obstacle course that no simulator can replicate.

I passed. License in hand. But honestly, that was the least memorable part of the entire trip.

The Culture Program

Outside of the drone training, the university had organized an incredible cultural program. We got to try traditional Chinese archery, martial arts, paddle sports, and — of course — table tennis. Playing table tennis with Chinese students hit different. The level, the intensity, the way they approach the sport. As someone who plays competitively in the Landesliga back home, this was pure gold.

Table tennis session at the university — competing with local players was an absolute highlight.

We also had workshops on traditional clothing, calligraphy, and Chinese music. These weren't surface-level tourist experiences — they were genuine, immersive sessions led by people who cared about sharing their culture.

Cultural workshop — creating traditional Chinese calligraphy and art. That's my piece on the right.

The Food

I'll be honest: I went in with some concerns. Different country, completely different cuisine, not sure how my stomach would handle it. Within 48 hours, those concerns were gone. The food was incredible. Every single meal. Breakfast, lunch, dinner — it didn't matter. The variety, the flavors, the sheer amount of food on the table at every sitting. I've never eaten this well for two straight weeks.

A typical dinner. And yes, every meal looked like this.

Chinese food culture is something else. It's not just about the food itself — it's about sharing, about sitting around a table together, about trying everything. That communal aspect of eating together became one of my favorite parts of the daily routine.

The People

This is the part that matters most. The part that actually changed something in me.

The level of hospitality I experienced in Shenzhen was unlike anything I've encountered before. And I don't say that lightly. The students, the faculty, random people we met — everyone was open, warm, genuinely curious about who we were and where we came from. The connections happened fast and naturally, without any of the awkwardness you sometimes get in cross-cultural situations.

With two of the local students I got closest to. The Shenzhen skyline at night is unreal.

I made friendships in those two weeks that felt like they'd been building for months. There's something about being in a completely foreign environment that strips away the small talk and gets you to the real stuff faster. Shared meals, shared training sessions, shared laughs about language barriers — it all adds up quickly.

Part of our exchange group. Two weeks that felt like two months.

What I Took Home

A drone license, sure. But more importantly: a completely shifted perspective. I went to Shenzhen expecting a technical training program with some cultural extras on the side. What I got was an experience that made me rethink how I approach new environments, new people, and new cultures.

The biggest lesson was simple: openness is reciprocal. The more genuinely curious you are about someone else's world, the more they open up theirs to you. The Chinese students I met weren't just being polite — they were genuinely interested in exchange. And when that energy is mutual, the result is something you can't manufacture.

I've been to other countries. I'll go to more. But Shenzhen set a benchmark. For the intensity of the experience, for the depth of the connections, and for how much you can grow in just two weeks when you're fully immersed in something unfamiliar.

If you ever get the chance to do an exchange program — especially one that puts you somewhere you'd never normally go — take it. Don't overthink it. The things that scare you about it beforehand are usually the things that make it unforgettable.
